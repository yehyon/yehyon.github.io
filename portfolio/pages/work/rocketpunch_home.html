RocketPunch Home

The one stop business feed for your professional network

Overview

The objective of the project was to redesign the RocketPunch homepage to reflect our main mission: enabling our users to connect through our platform to create a strong professional network. There were many iterations which involved a design sprint failure.

However, through those trials and errors we were able to reach a final solution: a timeline formed by your professional network.

Lead Product Designer

Research, Information Architecture, Interaction, UI/UX Design & testing

Launched in 2018

Why We Decided to Redesign the Homepage

It was quite obvious that we had a problem. Our users were barely using our homepage. Data analytics showed that the typical user flow when someone lands on our homepage was to go straight to our secondary pages such as search or profiles pages .

The home page was serving no functionality aside from being a mere placeholder.

However, it took us many iterations and discussions to really understand why we weren’t able to nail a solid homepage that would bring value to our users.

Why We Chose to Do a Design Sprint (That failed)

As mentioned in the overview, we embarked on a design sprint in early 2018 in which we failed to figure out our eventual final solution. We knew we had to redesign the homepage, but there was so much uncertainty. Since 2017, RocketPunch was transitioning from a recruitment platform to a business networking platform.

Due to this major shift in our product identity, we were at a stage where as a company it was necessary for us to reassess our product goals and mission.

Thus, as a team we decided that a design sprint would be the best method to redesign a homepage that aligns with our new identity. The different stages of a design sprint forced us to face the difficult questions we were unable to answer.

Why this Sprint was Valuable Despite the Failure

We weren’t able to nail the final solution in this sprint. Our goal was to design a key user flow that will help our users better understand the value and mission of our product: creating professional connections through our platform.

Userflow from Design Sprint

Our original solution was centered around the idea of customized content curation. We wanted to motivate our users to connect with other users by curating a recommendation of people they would most likely know or be interested in. Thus, we designed a homepage that functions as a task inbox where people would receive new sets of recommendations of people or content in their inbox each day.

InVision Prototype in Action

Sadly, on Friday during user-testing we quickly realized that we have yet to nail a final solution. However, we were able to gain much valuable insight that eventually set us on the right path, which led to our final solution: a timeline.

Key Takeaways from Sprint Friday: User Interviews

1. Seeing the existence of other users is not enough to motivate users to connect with one another
2. Static profiles do not indicate whether the following users are actually active on our platform
3. Unless the users are active, there's little motivation to connect through our platform
4. The content needed better indication as to why they were being recommended
5. We needed a source of validation for the content we were recommending to our users

From these takeaways, we realized that we needed to shift our focus from content to people. Good content is always important. However, the solution to motivating our users to connect with one another through our platform relied on the people on our platform. It was about how can we make our users feel the dynamic presence of one another. Although we may not have been able to reach a final solution, we were able to identify the problem, set a clear goal on a solid path through our design sprint. Not it was time to start designing once again with what we’ve learned.

Starting Over

After the sprint, there was a few more rounds of rough sketches and whiteboarding but in the end we reached the final solution: design a timeline. A news feed was the solution that addressed all of our issues.

Unique Design Challenges

There were several unique design challenges during this project due to the nature of a feed. A feed is where you see the preview of all different kinds of content that originally belong in other parts of the product. They’re already designed and displayed a certain way. Thus, in order to bring them into the feed, I had to go through each data type in our product and create an alternative design that would work with the card format we used for our feed.

Challenge #1: The Sheer Number of Card Types

We have corporate personal profiles. We have jobs data. We have investment data etc... Going through all the different types of data we have and creating a card for each type that adheres to our design library was a challenge.

Project Takeaway

Poptip Questions was Poptip's very first product as well as the very first product I ever designed. Thus, every single step was a valuable takeaway. However, in hindsight as an experienced product designer I see that it was a unique opportunity to work with dynamic data. Because the data flow was constant yet unpredictable it was very important to design for unforseen edge cases.

Shoot for Simplicity and Clarity

Simplicity and clarity are the best ways to avoid mishappenings. Whether it be content or visual embelishment, Poptip Questions is the product that made me develop a habit of questioning myself whether an element is truely necessary, which also comes with the benifit of lowering engineering cost.
